# Types of Sentences (Grades 3-5)

## What is a Sentence?

A sentence is a group of words that expresses a complete thought. Every sentence needs two things:
1. A **subject** (who or what)
2. A **predicate** (what they do)

Example: **The dog** (subject) **ran fast** (predicate).

## The Four Types of Sentences

### 1. Declarative Sentences (Statements) üìù

**What they do:** Tell or state something
**Ending:** Period (.)
**Purpose:** Give information

**Examples:**
- The sky is blue.
- I love reading books.
- Cats are mammals.
- My birthday is in June.
- Pizza is my favorite food.

**How to recognize them:**
- They make a statement
- They end with a period
- They tell you something

### 2. Interrogative Sentences (Questions) ‚ùì

**What they do:** Ask questions
**Ending:** Question mark (?)
**Purpose:** Get information

**Examples:**
- What is your name?
- Where do you live?
- How old are you?
- Can you help me?
- Why is the sky blue?

**Question words:**
- Who, What, When, Where, Why, How, Which
- Do, Does, Did, Can, Could, Will, Would, Should

**How to recognize them:**
- They ask something
- They end with a question mark
- Usually start with a question word or helping verb

### 3. Imperative Sentences (Commands) üëâ

**What they do:** Give orders or make requests
**Ending:** Period (.) or Exclamation point (!)
**Purpose:** Tell someone to do something

**Examples:**
- Close the door.
- Please pass the salt.
- Sit down!
- Do your homework.
- Stop running in the hallway!

**Special feature:**
- Subject (you) is usually hidden!
- "(You) close the door" - "You" is understood

**How to recognize them:**
- They tell you to do something
- Subject is usually "you" (but not written)
- Can end with . or !

### 4. Exclamatory Sentences (Exclamations) ‚ùó

**What they do:** Show strong emotion or excitement
**Ending:** Exclamation point (!)
**Purpose:** Express strong feelings

**Examples:**
- What a beautiful day!
- I can't believe we won!
- That's amazing!
- Wow, look at that huge elephant!
- I'm so happy to see you!

**Emotions they show:**
- Excitement, surprise, joy, anger, fear

**How to recognize them:**
- Show strong emotion
- End with exclamation point
- Often start with "What" or "How"

## Comparing the Types

**Same topic, different sentence types:**

- **Declarative:** The game is exciting.
- **Interrogative:** Is the game exciting?
- **Imperative:** Watch the game.
- **Exclamatory:** What an exciting game!

**Another example:**

- **Declarative:** You are a good friend.
- **Interrogative:** Are you a good friend?
- **Imperative:** Be a good friend.
- **Exclamatory:** What a good friend you are!

## Parts of a Sentence

### Subject
**What it is:** Who or what the sentence is about

**Examples:**
- **The cat** jumped on the table.
- **My sister** loves chocolate.
- **Thunder** is very loud.

**How to find it:** Ask "Who or what is doing something?"

### Predicate
**What it is:** What the subject does or is

**Examples:**
- The cat **jumped on the table**.
- My sister **loves chocolate**.
- Thunder **is very loud**.

**How to find it:** Ask "What is the subject doing?" or "What is being said about the subject?"

## Simple vs. Compound Sentences

### Simple Sentence
**What it is:** One independent thought

**Examples:**
- I like ice cream.
- The dog barked.
- She runs every day.

**Structure:** One subject + one predicate

### Compound Sentence
**What it is:** Two or more simple sentences joined together

**Examples:**
- I like ice cream, and she likes cake.
- The dog barked, but nobody heard it.
- We can go to the park, or we can stay home.

**How to make them:**
- Use FANBOYS (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so)
- Put a comma before the conjunction

## Sentence Fragments vs. Complete Sentences

### Complete Sentence ‚úì
Has both subject and predicate

**Examples:**
- The bird flew away.
- I am happy.
- Dogs bark loudly.

### Fragment ‚úó
Missing subject or predicate (incomplete thought)

**Examples of fragments:**
- Ran to the store. (Who ran?)
- The big brown dog. (What about the dog?)
- After we finished. (What happened?)

**How to fix fragments:**
- Add the missing part!
- Ran to the store ‚Üí **I** ran to the store.
- The big brown dog ‚Üí The big brown dog **barked**.
- After we finished ‚Üí After we finished, **we went home**.

## Run-On Sentences

**What they are:** Two or more sentences incorrectly joined without proper punctuation

**Examples of run-ons:**
- ‚úó I like pizza I also like burgers.
- ‚úó The sun is shining it's a beautiful day.
- ‚úó She ran fast she won the race.

**How to fix them:**

1. **Add a period:**
   - I like pizza**.** I also like burgers.

2. **Add a comma and conjunction:**
   - I like pizza**, and** I also like burgers.

3. **Add a semicolon:**
   - I like pizza**;** I also like burgers.

## Punctuation Rules

### Period (.)
- End of declarative sentences
- End of imperative sentences
- After abbreviations (Dr., Mr., etc.)

### Question Mark (?)
- End of interrogative sentences
- Shows you're asking something

### Exclamation Point (!)
- End of exclamatory sentences
- End of strong imperative sentences
- Shows strong emotion

**Important:** Don't overuse exclamation points! One is enough!

## Capital Letters

**Always capitalize:**
1. **First word** of every sentence
2. **Proper nouns** (names, places)
3. **The pronoun "I"**
4. **Titles** (Mr., Mrs., Dr.)
5. **Days and months** (Monday, January)

**Examples:**
- **T**he cat is sleeping.
- **M**y name is **S**arah.
- **I** live in **N**ew **Y**ork.
- **M**r. **J**ones is my teacher.

## Writing Better Sentences

### Tips for Strong Sentences:

1. **Vary sentence types** - Don't use all statements!
2. **Start sentences differently** - Not always "The..."
3. **Use specific words** - "sprinted" instead of "ran fast"
4. **Combine short sentences** - Make compound sentences
5. **Check for fragments** - Every sentence needs subject + predicate

### Examples of Improvement:

**Before (boring):**
The dog ran. The dog was fast. The dog caught the ball.

**After (better):**
The fast dog sprinted across the yard and caught the ball!

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Forgetting end punctuation**
   - ‚úó I like school
   - ‚úì I like school.

2. **Wrong punctuation mark**
   - ‚úó Where are you going.
   - ‚úì Where are you going?

3. **Sentence fragments**
   - ‚úó Because it was raining.
   - ‚úì We stayed inside because it was raining.

4. **Run-on sentences**
   - ‚úó I'm hungry let's eat.
   - ‚úì I'm hungry, so let's eat.

## Key Vocabulary

- **Declarative**: Makes a statement
- **Interrogative**: Asks a question
- **Imperative**: Gives a command
- **Exclamatory**: Shows strong emotion
- **Subject**: Who or what the sentence is about
- **Predicate**: What the subject does or is
- **Fragment**: Incomplete sentence
- **Run-on**: Sentences incorrectly joined

## Remember:

Every sentence needs:
- A subject (who/what)
- A predicate (does/is)
- A capital letter at the start
- Correct punctuation at the end

Practice writing different types of sentences to make your writing more interesting and engaging!
